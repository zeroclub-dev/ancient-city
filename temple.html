Lyre (Chelys)

The lyre was the most beloved string instrument of ancient Greece, associated with Apollo and the Muses. Made from a tortoise shell with arms of wood or horn, it typically had seven strings. The lyre was played by strumming with a plectrum while fingers of the other hand dampened unwanted strings.

Kithara

The kithara was a professional version of the lyre with a wooden sound box. Used by skilled musicians called kitharodes, it featured in contests and had a deeper, more resonant sound than the lyre. Apollo was often depicted bearing this prestigious instrument.

Aulos (Double Pipes)

The aulos consisted of two pipes with reed mouthpieces played simultaneously. Its powerful, penetrating sound featured in religious rituals, theater performances, and even on the battlefield. The aulos had a reputation for evoking strong emotions and was associated with the worship of Dionysus.

Syrinx (Pan Flute)

The syrinx consisted of graduated pipes bound together, each producing a single note when blown across. Associated with the god Pan and shepherds, it produced a sweet, breathy sound ideal for pastoral settings. According to myth, it was created by Pan from reeds into which the nymph Syrinx had been transformed.

Tympanon (Frame Drum)

The tympanon was a handheld drum used particularly in ecstatic religious rites dedicated to Dionysus and Cybele. It consisted of a wooden hoop with stretched animal hide. Often played by women, its deep, resonant sound helped induce trance-like states during festivals and processions.

Hydraulis (Water Organ)

Invented in the 3rd century BCE by Ctesibius of Alexandria, the hydraulis was the world's first keyboard instrument. It used water pressure to regulate airflow through a series of pipes, creating a powerful, sustained sound. This remarkable feat of engineering bridged music and science, demonstrating Greek innovation.
